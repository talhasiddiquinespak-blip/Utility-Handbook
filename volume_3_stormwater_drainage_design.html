1. Catchment Delineation & Topologic Breakdown

Before any mathematical method is applied, a site must be correctly delineated into sub-catchments. Modern software (like SWMM, SewerGEMS, or InfoDrainage) requires a strict nodal topology. You cannot simply apply one massive area to a single outfall if you want an accurate pipe network.

The Node-Link-Subcatchment Model
  • Node (Inlet/Manhole): The entry point of runoff into the buried system.
  • Link (Pipe/Channel): The hydraulic conduit transferring water between nodes.
  • Subcatchment: The specific topographic area draining exclusively to a single Node.
Delineation Rules (Civil 3D / GIS)
Polygons are drawn using proposed grading contours. The ridge lines dictate flow direction. Every square meter of the site must be assigned to an inlet node.
Width Parameter (SWMM specific)
In EPA SWMM, subcatchment "Width" is critical. $W = \text{Area} / \text{Length of overland flow}$. It dictates how quickly runoff enters the node.

2. Advanced Hydrology Methods

Interviews for principal roles will test your knowledge beyond the basic Rational equation. You must understand when to apply peak flow methods versus hydrograph methods.

2.1 Rational & Modified Rational Method
The Rational Equation (Peak Flow Only)
$$Q_p = \frac{C \cdot i \cdot A}{360}$$
Where: $Q_p$ = Peak Flow ($m^3/s$), $C$ = Runoff Coefficient, $i$ = Rainfall Intensity ($mm/hr$), $A$ = Area (Hectares). Limit: $A \le 80 \text{ Ha}$.
Modified Rational Method (For Volume/Pond Sizing)

Unlike the standard method, this creates a trapezoidal hydrograph. It assumes the storm duration ($T_d$) is longer than the time of concentration ($T_c$). The engineer iterates different storm durations to find the one that produces the maximum required storage volume ($V_{max}$).

$$V_{storage} = \left( \frac{C \cdot i(T_d) \cdot A}{360} - Q_{allowable} \right) \cdot T_d \cdot 60$$
2.2 SCS Curve Number & Unit Hydrograph (TR-55 / HEC-HMS)

Mandatory for catchments $> 80$ Hectares or when exact flow-over-time (hydrograph) routing is required. It separates abstractions (infiltration/depression storage) from effective rainfall.

Runoff Depth ($Q$) and Storage ($S$)
$$Q = \frac{(P - I_a)^2}{(P - I_a) + S}$$
$$S = \frac{25400}{CN} - 254 \quad \text{(Metric, mm)}$$
Where: $P$ = Total Rainfall Depth (mm), $I_a$ = Initial Abstraction (Usually $0.2S$), $CN$ = Curve Number (30 to 98).
SCS Dimensionless Unit Hydrograph

Converts the runoff depth ($Q$) into a time-based hydrograph using the time to peak ($T_p$).

$$T_p = \frac{\Delta D}{2} + L_g$$
Where: $\Delta D$ = Rainfall burst duration, $L_g$ = Basin Lag Time (often approximated as $0.6 \cdot T_c$). Peak discharge $q_p = \frac{2.08 \cdot A \cdot Q}{T_p}$.
2.3 EPA SWMM Non-Linear Reservoir Method

This is how hydraulic software (InfoWorks, SewerGEMS, EPA SWMM) actually generates runoff internally. It treats every subcatchment as a rectangular reservoir.

  • Inflow: Precipitation.
  • Outflow: Evaporation + Infiltration (via Green-Ampt or Horton equations) + Surface Runoff.
  • The Runoff Equation: Uses Manning's equation for overland flow once depression storage ($d_p$) is exceeded.
    $$Q = \frac{W}{n} \cdot (d - d_p)^{5/3} \cdot S^{1/2}$$
    Where: $W$ = Subcatchment Width, $n$ = Overland Manning's roughness, $d$ = Water depth, $S$ = Slope.

3. Time of Concentration ($T_c$) & IDF Application

The time required for runoff to travel from the hydraulically most distant point to the node. $T_c$ directly controls the intensity ($i$) pulled from the Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) curve.

Method Equation / Concept Best Application
Kirpich Equation $T_c = 0.0195 \cdot L^{0.77} \cdot S^{-0.385}$ Natural, unpaved, steep basins. Tends to vastly underestimate $T_c$ in flat, paved urban areas.
FAA Method $T_c = \frac{1.8 \cdot (1.1 - C) \cdot L^{0.5}}{S^{0.33}}$ Urban airfields and highly paved flat commercial sites.
NRCS Velocity (TR-55) $T_c = T_{\text{sheet}} + T_{\text{shallow}} + T_{\text{channel}}$ The absolute industry standard. Calculates travel time segment by segment based on actual ground cover and slope.

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: Why do municipalities mandate a "Minimum $T_c$" (usually 5 to 10 minutes)?
Answer: On a theoretical IDF curve, as Duration approaches zero, Intensity ($i$) approaches infinity. For a tiny roof catchment, calculating a $T_c$ of 30 seconds would yield a mathematically absurd rainfall intensity (e.g., $400 \text{ mm/hr}$), leading to wildly oversized pipes. A minimum 10-minute $T_c$ caps the intensity, representing the physical reality that it takes time for roofs to wet, minor depressions to fill, and initial splashing to organize into channelized overland flow.

4. Software Hydraulic Routing (The Software Black Box)

When you press "Run" in SewerGEMS, InfoDrainage, or SWMM, the software routes the hydrographs through the pipe network. Interviewers want to know that you understand the mathematical engine you selected in the settings.

A. Steady State Routing

Translates peak flows instantly from node to node. Used for basic Rational Method pipe sizing. Ignores volume, ignores time, ignores backwater.

B. Kinematic Wave Routing

Solves the continuity equation and a simplified momentum equation. Hydrographs move through pipes and can attenuate (flatten out), but it cannot model backwater effects, pressurized surcharging, or reverse flow. If the outfall is submerged, Kinematic Wave will fail to show the resulting upstream flooding.

C. Dynamic Wave Routing (St. Venant Equations)

The gold standard. Solves the complete 1D Saint-Venant equations (Continuity + full Momentum). Accounts for channel storage, backwater, entrance/exit losses, flow reversal, and pressurized surcharging. Always use this for flat terrain, complex interconnected ponds, or tidal outfalls.

5. Detention Pond Level-Pool Routing

When post-development flow exceeds allowable pre-development discharge, a detention basin is required. Ponds are routed using the Continuity Equation for Storage:

$$\frac{\Delta S}{\Delta t} = I_{avg} - O_{avg}$$

Change in Storage over time = Average Inflow - Average Outflow

The software establishes a Stage-Storage-Discharge relationship. The engineer inputs the pond contour areas (Stage vs. Storage) and designs an outlet structure (e.g., a multi-stage weir/orifice combination) to define the Stage vs. Discharge curve.

  • Orifice Flow (Low flow throttle): $Q = C_d \cdot A \cdot \sqrt{2gH}$
  • Weir Flow (High flow overflow): $Q = C_w \cdot L \cdot H^{3/2}$

6. REAL-LIFE CASE STUDY: From Hydrology to IFC

Project: "Apex Logistics Hub" - Full SWMM Execution

The Brief: You are the Lead Wet Utilities Engineer for a 12-hectare industrial logistics park. The municipality dictates that post-development peak discharge must not exceed pre-development peak discharge for the 10-year storm event. The downstream outfall is partially submerged by a river.

Step 1: Topologic Catchment Breaking & Pre-Dev Hydrology

You cannot treat the 12-Ha site as one node. You overlay the proposed grading plan in Civil 3D and delineate 8 distinct subcatchments, each draining to a specific catch basin. But first, we find the global allowable limit.

  • Pre-Dev Surface: Pasture/Grassland. Composite Runoff Coefficient ($C_{pre}$) = $0.20$.
  • $T_c$: Using TR-55, $T_c$ = $30 \text{ minutes}$.
  • Rainfall Intensity ($i$): From IDF curve (10-yr, 30-min) = $50 \text{ mm/hr}$.
  • Target Outflow Limit: $$Q_{pre} = \frac{C \cdot i \cdot A}{360} = \frac{0.20 \cdot 50 \cdot 12}{360} = \mathbf{0.33 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}}$$
Step 2: Post-Development Load & Dynamic Software Setup

The site is now 90% paved asphalt yards ($C_{post} = 0.85$). We build the network in EPA SWMM.

  • Because the downstream river outfall is submerged, Kinematic Wave routing will yield false results. We select Dynamic Wave Routing in SWMM settings to accurately model the backwater effect pushing up the pipes.
  • We apply an SCS Type II 24-hour design storm hyetograph to generate inflow hydrographs for all 8 subcatchments.
  • The unmitigated post-development peak flow entering the system is modeled at **$2.27 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}$**.
Step 3: Internal Trunk Sewer Sizing (Manning's Verification)

We check Subcatchment 4's outfall pipe. Topography restricts slope ($S$) to $0.005 \text{ m/m}$.

  • Trial: 900mm ($0.9\text{m}$) RCP Pipe (n=0.013)
    • $A = \pi \cdot (0.45)^2 = 0.636 \text{ m}^2$
    • $R = D/4 = 0.225 \text{ m}$
    • $V = \frac{1}{0.013} \cdot (0.225)^{2/3} \cdot (0.005)^{1/2} = \mathbf{2.01 \text{ m/s}}$
    • $Q_{cap} = A \cdot V = 0.636 \cdot 2.01 = \mathbf{1.28 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}}$
    • SWMM Output check: The Dynamic Wave profile shows the HGL breaching the manhole rim. The 900mm pipe is undersized for the $2.27 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}$ peak.
  • Revision: 1200mm ($1.2\text{m}$) Pipe handles $2.75 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}$. We upsize the trunk main to 1200mm. The HGL drops safely below the surface.
Step 4: Detention Basin & Multi-Stage Outlet Design

We must throttle the $2.27 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}$ back down to the allowable $0.33 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}$. We design a dry detention basin.

  • Storage Volume: SWMM routing indicates a required volume of $4,500 \text{ m}^3$ to attenuate the peak.
  • Outlet Structure: To restrict flow to $0.33 \text{ m}^3\text{/s}$ under a maximum pond head ($H$) of $1.5\text{m}$, we size a bottom orifice. $$Q = C_d \cdot A \cdot \sqrt{2gH}$$ $$0.33 = 0.60 \cdot A \cdot \sqrt{2 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 1.5}$$ $$0.33 = 0.60 \cdot A \cdot 5.42 \implies A = 0.101 \text{ m}^2$$
  • Solving for diameter: $A = \frac{\pi \cdot D^2}{4} \implies D = \mathbf{360 \text{ mm}}$. We specify a $350\text{mm}$ control orifice plate on the outfall structure.
Step 5: IFC & Clash Detection

The 1200mm trunk intersects a proposed sanitary sewer. The storm invert is $-2.5\text{m}$. The sanitary crown is $-3.2\text{m}$. Separation is $0.7\text{m}$, safely passing the 0.3m (12-inch) code minimum. The design is locked for Issued for Construction (IFC).
