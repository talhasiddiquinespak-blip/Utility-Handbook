1. Wastewater Generation & Base Flows

Unlike water distribution (pressure flow), sanitary sewers are gravity-driven open-channel systems. Estimating the correct base flow is the critical first step before applying peaking factors or calculating pipe hydraulics.

Governing Standards Ten States Part 10 Metcalf & Eddy (5th Ed) BS EN 752 Sewers for Adoption (UK)
Dry Weather Flow (DWF)The baseline flow consisting of domestic sewage, commercial/industrial effluent, and unavoidable baseline groundwater infiltration (no storm influence).
Peak Wet Weather Flow (PWWF)The ultimate design flow. Consists of Peak DWF + Extreme Infiltration & Inflow (I&I) from storm events. Pipes are strictly sized for PWWF.

1.1 Flow Estimation Methods

A. Water Demand / Return Factor Method (Most Common)
Equation: $$DWF = Population \times Per\_Capita\_Demand \times Return\_Factor$$
Return Factor: Typically 0.75 to 0.85 (75% - 85%). The remaining 15-25% is lost to evaporation, landscape irrigation, and consumption.
Application: Used for large-scale master planning and municipal expansions where accurate water consumption data exists.
B. Unit Flow / Land Use Method
Concept: Assigning a specific flow rate per unit of land area (e.g., Liters/Hectare/day) based on zoning laws.
Typical Values: Light Industrial: 15,000 L/Ha/d. Heavy Industrial: 40,000+ L/Ha/d. Commercial: 25,000 L/Ha/d.
Application: Greenfield developments, industrial parks, and areas where population density is unknown but building footprints are fixed.
C. Fixture Unit Method
Concept: Based on the Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC) or International Plumbing Code (IPC). Each toilet, sink, and shower is assigned a "Drainage Fixture Unit" (DFU).
Application: Small developments, high-rise building connections, hospitals, or specific campus facilities. Converts total DFUs to GPM using Hunter's Curve.
Limitation: Highly conservative. Will drastically oversize municipal mains if applied to entire cities.

2. Peaking Factors and I&I

Sewage flow follows a diurnal pattern, peaking in the morning (showers) and evening (dinner/laundry). As the population grows, statistical diversity flattens the peak. Therefore, the smaller the population, the higher the peaking factor.

2.1 Peaking Factor Formulas

Method Equation Variables Usage / Notes
Harmon Formula $$PF = 1 + \frac{14}{4 + \sqrt{P}}$$ P = Population in thousands (e.g., for 5,000 pax, P = 5). The global gold standard (Metcalf & Eddy). Capped at a max of 4.0 or 5.0 in most codes. Drops to ~2.0 for very large cities.
Babbit Formula $$PF = \frac{5}{P^{0.2}}$$ P = Population in thousands. Yields slightly more conservative (higher) peaks than Harmon. Common in US. Max limit typically 5.0.
Ten States Constant Nomograph / Constant 4.0 Depends on flow rate, lateral vs trunk. Ten States §11.24 states peak hourly flow should be based on local data, but often dictates a constant PF of 4.0 for laterals.
BS EN 752 / UK $$PWWF = 6 \times DWF$$ Constant multiplier. UK standard often dictates sizing sewers for 6x DWF (which accounts for both domestic peaking and significant inflow).

2.2 Infiltration & Inflow (I&I)

Infiltration: Groundwater seeping into the sewer through defective pipe joints, cracked pipes, or bad manhole seals.
Inflow: Surface stormwater illegally or accidentally entering the sewer (e.g., roof drains connected to sanitary, perforated manhole covers in sags).

Common Mistake: Failing to add an I&I allowance to a new PVC/HDPE system because "the joints are watertight." Ten States mandates I&I allowances regardless of material. Manholes settle, contractors make mistakes, and illegal connections occur over a 50-year design life. Typical allowances are 10% to 20% of Peak Flow or 2,000 to 10,000 Liters/Hectare/day.

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: You are designing a sewer for an industrial zone that operates 24/7 with a constant process discharge of 50 L/s. Do you apply the Harmon peaking factor to this flow?
Answer: Absolutely not. The Harmon formula is derived from the statistical diurnal habits of human populations (sleeping, waking, showering). It does not apply to continuous industrial process water. I would add the raw 50 L/s industrial flow directly to the already peaked domestic flow of the surrounding area.

3. Sewer Pipe Material Selection

Unlike water mains, sewers operate at atmospheric pressure but contain highly corrosive Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S) gas. Therefore, chemical resistance is often prioritized over pressure rating.

The H₂S Problem: In sluggish, warm sewage, anaerobic bacteria reduce sulfates into H₂S gas. This gas rises to the pipe crown, where Thiobacillus bacteria convert it into Sulfuric Acid $(H_2SO_4)$. This acid aggressively eats alkaline materials (like bare concrete).
Material Pros Cons / Failure Modes Best For
uPVC (Solid Wall) Immune to H₂S, extremely smooth (Manning's n=0.009), lightweight. Becomes brittle over time, deflections if trench compaction is poor. Max sizes usually limited to ~600mm. Gravity laterals, residential subdivision networks.
HDPE (Corrugated or Solid) Immune to H₂S, flexible (resists earth movement), welded joints stop infiltration. Requires skilled welding, susceptible to floatation in high water tables if backfill is incorrect. Trenchless applications, high groundwater areas, force mains.
Vitrified Clay Pipe (VCP) The absolute highest chemical resistance. Incredible lifespan (100+ years). Very brittle, heavy, short pipe lengths mean many joints (high risk of root intrusion/infiltration). Highly aggressive industrial waste, deep urban trenches.
Reinforced Concrete Pipe (RCP) Massive structural strength for deep burial, available in huge diameters (>3000mm). Highly susceptible to H₂S crown corrosion. MUST be lined with PVC/HDPE ribs or epoxy in sanitary applications. Deep trunk sewers, massive interceptors.

4. Hydraulic Design of Gravity Sewers

Sewers are open channels. Flow is driven entirely by the pipe's slope. The universal governing equation is the Manning Equation.

Manning's Equation
$$V = \frac{1}{n} \cdot R^{2/3} \cdot S^{1/2}$$
$$Q = A \cdot V = \frac{A}{n} \cdot R^{2/3} \cdot S^{1/2}$$
Where: V = Velocity (m/s), n = Roughness Coeff (dimensionless), R = Hydraulic Radius (A/P in meters), S = Slope (m/m), A = Wetted Area.
Design Roughness (n)
Ten States strictly requires using n = 0.013 for ALL pipe materials during design to account for future slime buildup, even if PVC is 0.009 in the factory.
Minimum Diameter
Ten States § 33.1 requires a minimum of 200 mm (8 inches) for municipal gravity sewers to prevent blockage from rags/FOG.

4.1 Velocity and Slope Constraints

Sewer slopes must be perfectly balanced. Too flat, and solids drop out. Too steep, and the water outruns the solids or scours the pipe invert.

  • Self-Cleansing Velocity (Min): 0.6 m/s (2.0 ft/s) when flowing full or half-full. Achieved via minimum slopes (e.g., 0.40% for 200mm).
  • Maximum Velocity: 3.0 m/s (10.0 ft/s). Higher velocities cause grit to erode the pipe invert. If topography is too steep, introduce Drop Manholes to break the slope.
  • d/D Ratio (Depth of Flow): Sewers are NEVER designed to flow 100% full. Maximum allowable d/D is typically 0.50 for pipes < 300mm, and 0.70 for pipes > 400mm. This empty space ensures ventilation of H₂S gases and provides a buffer for extreme storm inflow.

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: A 200mm sewer pipe is designed at a slope of 0.5%, giving a full-pipe velocity of 0.7 m/s (passing the 0.6 m/s rule). However, during initial occupancy, the actual flow is only 10% of the design capacity. Will solids deposit?
Answer: Yes, absolutely. This is the "Partial Flow" trap. While the full-bore velocity is 0.7 m/s, at a d/D ratio of 0.10 (very shallow water), the hydraulic radius drops significantly. According to the partial flow hydraulic elements graph, the actual velocity will drop below 0.3 m/s. Solids will strand. To fix this, I must either steepen the initial slope to achieve 0.6 m/s at the actual partial flow, or implement a flushing program until the development builds out.

5. Manholes and Lift Stations

5.1 Manhole Design Criteria

  • Spacing: Max 120m (400 ft) for pipes under 400mm. Max 150m (500 ft) for larger pipes. Reason: Limits of CCTV inspection and hydro-jetting cleaning cables.
  • Locations: Placed at EVERY change in grade, change in size, change in alignment, and at all intersections.
  • Drop Manholes: Required when an incoming sewer invert is $\ge 0.6m$ (24 inches) above the manhole invert. An external/internal drop pipe routes the flow down. *Why? To protect workers from splashing, prevent structural scour of the manhole benching, and prevent turbulent stripping of H₂S gas into the atmosphere.*

5.2 Lift Station & Wet Well Sizing

When excavation depth exceeds 6-8 meters (economic limit of trenching) or water must cross a ridge, a pump station is required.

Active Volume Equation (Ten States § 42.6):
$$V = \frac{T_{min} \cdot q}{4}$$
Where: V = Active Volume between Pump ON and OFF levels. $T_{min}$ = Minimum pump cycle time (typically 10-15 minutes to prevent motor burnout). q = Pump capacity (usually sized to handle PWWF).
Redundancy: Must have at least two pumps (1 Duty, 1 Standby). If Duty fails, Standby handles 100% of PWWF.
Force Mains: Minimum velocity 0.9 m/s (higher than gravity to keep solids suspended vertically). Max velocity 2.5 m/s (prevent severe water hammer on pump trip).

6. Final Deliverables (IFC)

The Issued for Construction (IFC) package for sewers requires extreme precision in invert elevations. A 10cm mistake in a water pressure pipe is negligible; a 10cm mistake in a gravity sewer creates a sag, traps solids, and ruins the network.

  • Plan and Profile: Must show crossing utilities. Minimum separation per Ten States: Water mains must be 10 feet horizontal and 18 inches vertical (water above sewer) from any sanitary line.
  • Trench Details: Bedding classes (e.g., granular envelop to springline) to prevent pipe deflection under traffic loads.

7. REAL-LIFE CASE STUDY: From Master Plan to IFC

This section walks through a complete senior-level design execution, mimicking a technical interview test or a real consulting project workflow.

Project: "Oasis Greens" Residential Subdivision

The Brief: You are the Lead Wet Utilities Engineer. The client is building a 500-villa luxury subdivision on a 25-hectare flat site. You must design the sanitary network and connect it to an existing municipal trunk sewer located 1 km away. The existing trunk invert is at Depth = -2.0m.

Step 1: Establish Design Criteria & Flows

  • Population: 500 villas $\times$ 5.5 capita/villa = 2,750 people.
  • Per Capita Demand: 250 Liters/capita/day (Luxury sizing).
  • Sewage Generation (Return Factor): $250 \times 0.80 = 200$ L/c/d.
  • Average Dry Weather Flow (ADWF): $2,750 \times 200 = 550,000$ L/day = 6.36 L/s.

Step 2: Peaking and I&I

  • Peaking Factor (Harmon): $PF = 1 + \frac{14}{4 + \sqrt{2.75}} = 1 + 2.47 =$ 3.47
  • Peak Domestic Flow: $6.36 \text{ L/s} \times 3.47 = $ 22.0 L/s
  • I&I Allowance: (Assuming 10% of Peak for a well-sealed new uPVC network) = 2.2 L/s.
  • Total Design PWWF: 22.0 + 2.2 = 24.2 L/s. (The network outfall must handle this).

Step 3: Network Routing & Hydraulic Sizing (Manning's)

You begin routing from the furthest upstream node. By Ten States rules, you select the minimum allowable pipe size: 200mm uPVC (n=0.013).

  • To achieve the minimum self-cleansing velocity of 0.6 m/s, you calculate the minimum slope for a 200mm pipe: 0.33% (or 1 in 300).
  • At 0.33% slope, a 200mm pipe flowing full handles ~19 L/s.
  • Conflict! The outfall flow is 24.2 L/s. Therefore, the downstream collector mains must be upsized to 250mm.
  • At 0.33% slope, a 250mm pipe flowing full handles ~34 L/s. This safely passes the 24.2 L/s flow with a healthy d/D ratio of ~0.75, allowing gas ventilation.

Step 4: The Elevation Trap (The Lift Station Pivot)

You draw the profile. The site is perfectly flat. The furthest node starts at 1.2m depth (to allow house connections). The network runs for 800m at a 0.33% slope.

  • Elevation Drop in Network: $800\text{m} \times 0.0033 = 2.64\text{m}$ drop.
  • Outfall Depth: Start Depth (1.2m) + Drop (2.64m) = 3.84m deep at the edge of the site.
  • The Problem: The existing municipal trunk is only 2.0m deep. Gravity tie-in is physically impossible. Water cannot flow uphill.

The Solution: You must insert a Lift Station at the edge of the subdivision to pump the 24.2 L/s flow up to the municipal trunk.

Step 5: Lift Station & Force Main Design

  • Pumps: You select Two (2) Submersible Pumps (1 Duty, 1 Standby) rated for 25 L/s at 5 meters of Total Dynamic Head (Static lift + friction).
  • Force Main Sizing: To pass 25 L/s at an optimal velocity of ~1.2 m/s, you calculate $A = Q/V$. A 150mm DI (Ductile Iron) force main provides exactly 1.4 m/s. Perfect.
  • Wet Well Sizing: You use the Ten States rule to prevent the pump from turning on/off too rapidly (which burns out the motor). Max 4 starts per hour (15 min cycle).
    $V = \frac{T \cdot q}{4} = \frac{15 \text{ min} \cdot 1.5 \text{ m}^3/\text{min}}{4} =$ 5.625 m³ of Active Volume required between the floats.

Step 6: IFC Deliverables & Clash Detection

Before issuing the drawings to the contractor, you review the profiles. At station 0+400, the 250mm gravity sewer crosses beneath a 300mm water transmission main.

Code Check: You verify the vertical separation is 500mm (Code requires minimum 450mm / 18 inches). The crossing passes inspection. You finalize the BOQ and issue the package.
