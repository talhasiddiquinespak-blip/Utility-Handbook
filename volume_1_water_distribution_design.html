1. Design Criteria Establishment

The foundation of any municipal water network. Establishing criteria involves defining the hydraulic constraints, demand parameters, and regulatory frameworks before a single pipe is drawn.

PurposeTo ensure the designed network meets regulatory, performance, and safety requirements throughout its design life.
Required InputsTopography, Master Plan, local regulations, environmental constraints, fire authority mandates.
Governing Standards Ten States Part 8 AWWA M32 WHO GDWQ BS EN 805 CPHEEO Ch 2
Design AssumptionsSystem must provide domestic peak demand concurrently with fire flow. Minimum pressure must be maintained at highest elevation nodes.
Typical ValuesMin Velocity: 0.6 m/s (Ten States). Min Pressure: 20 psi / 1.4 bar during fire flow, 35-40 psi / 2.5 bar normal (AWWA). Max Velocity: 1.5 - 2.5 m/s.
Common Mistake: Establishing a uniform minimum pressure criteria without accounting for high-rise buildings (which require booster pumps rather than over-pressurizing the municipal mains) or failing to separate pressure zones where elevation varies by more than 30-40 meters.

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: An urban network has an elevation difference of 60 meters. How do you handle design criteria?
Answer: A 60m drop creates approx. 6 bars (87 psi) of static pressure difference. If the high zone requires a minimum of 2.5 bars, the low zone will experience 8.5 bars, exceeding typical allowable limits (often 6-7 bars) causing bursts and leakage. I would establish dual pressure zones using PRVs (Pressure Reducing Valves) or separate service reservoirs to keep operating pressures within the 2.5 to 6.0 bar sweet spot (BS EN 805 / AWWA standards).

2. Population Projection Methods

Accurate projection determines the ultimate capacity. Oversizing leads to water age and stagnation issues (chlorine decay); undersizing leads to premature hydraulic failure.

2.1 Arithmetic Increase Method
PurposeForecast population for old, established, fully built-out cities.
Equation
$$P_n = P_0 + n \cdot C$$

Pn = Future pop, P0 = Present pop, n = decades, C = avg increase per decade.
Governing StandardsCPHEEO Ch 2 Twort's Water Supply
When to useLarge, old cities with no new land for expansion.
When NOT to useNew developments or rapidly expanding suburbs.
AdvantagesSimple, prevents gross overestimation.
LimitationsIgnores compounding growth; purely linear.
2.2 Geometric Increase Method
PurposeForecast for rapidly growing, newly developing cities.
Equation
$$P_n = P_0 \left(1 + \frac{r}{100}\right)^n$$

r = average percentage growth rate per decade/year.
Governing StandardsTen States Part 1 CPHEEO
When to useCities with unlimited expansion potential and booming economies.
When NOT to useOld cities (will drastically over-predict demand).
LimitationsCompounding nature can result in absurdly high figures over 30+ year horizons.
2.3 Incremental Increase Method
PurposeA compromise between Arithmetic and Geometric methods.
Equation
$$P_n = P_0 + n \cdot X + \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \cdot Y$$

X = avg arithmetic increase, Y = avg incremental increase (change in X).
When to useCities currently growing but approaching saturation. Often mandated in South Asian (CPHEEO) master plans.
2.4 Logistic Growth (S-Curve) Method
PurposeMathematical modeling of population hitting a geographic/resource saturation limit.
Equation
$$P_t = \frac{P_{sat}}{1 + e^{a + b \Delta t}}$$

Psat = saturation population, a,b = constants derived from historical data.
When to useLong-term master planning (50+ years) where geographic boundaries are strictly defined.
2.5 Master Plan / Zoning Method
PurposeProjection based on statutory town planning limits.
MethodArea of Zone × Permitted Density (e.g., 50 Ha × 200 persons/Ha = 10,000 pop).
When to useGreenfield projects, planned subdivisions. *This is the most common method for specific real estate developments.*
2.6 Utility Connection Method
PurposeProjection based on active water/power meters.
EquationPop = (Number of active domestic meters) × (Average household size, e.g., 5.5).
AdvantagesHighly accurate baseline data; ignores census flaws.
2.7 Land Use Method
PurposeDemand driven by commercial/industrial footprint rather than capita.
MethodArea × Equivalent Demand (e.g., L/Ha/day for industrial zones).
Governing StandardsAWWA M1
2.8 Equivalent Population Method
PurposeConverting industrial/commercial loads into a "population equivalent" (PE) for standardized network modeling.
MethodPE = (Total Non-Domestic Demand in L/d) / (Per Capita Demand in L/c/d).
AdvantagesSimplifies EPANET demand allocation.

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: You are designing a 30-year master plan for a greenfield city that currently has zero population. Which projection method do you use and why?
Answer: I cannot use Arithmetic, Geometric, or Incremental methods because P = 0 and there is no historical census data. I must use the Master Plan / Land Use Method. I will take the urban planner's zoning map, apply housing densities (e.g., Low Density = 50 persons/Ha, High Density = 300 persons/Ha) to determine the ultimate saturation population, and phase the growth based on the developer's financial absorption rate.

3. Demand Calculations

Water systems are not designed for "average" conditions. They are designed for extremes. Understanding peaking factors is critical to sizing pipes, pumps, and tanks.

Demand Type Equation / Definition Typical Values / Peaking Factor Governing Code / Standard
Average Day Demand (ADD) ADD = Population × Per Capita Consumption + System Losses 150 - 250 L/c/d (Developed). 100 L/c/d (Rural). Loss: 10-20% WHO CPHEEO
Maximum Day Demand (MDD) MDD = ADD × Maximum Day Peaking Factor PF = 1.5 to 1.8. (e.g. hottest day of the year). Ten States § 8.1 AWWA M32
Peak Hour Demand (PHD) PHD = MDD × Peak Hour Factor OR ADD × Combined PF Combined PF = 2.25 to 3.0 (Highest hour on the highest day). BS EN 805
Fire Flow Demand US ISO Formula: $Q = 220 \sqrt{P} \left(1 - 0.01\sqrt{P}\right)$gpm (P=pop in thousands) Residential: 1,000 - 1,500 gpm. Comm/Ind: 2,500 - 3,500 gpm. AWWA M31 NFPA 1
Combined Demand Design Flow = MAX (PHD) OR (MDD + Fire Flow) Mains must be sized for the larger of these two scenarios. Ten States § 8.1.1
Industrial Demand Liters per hectare per day (L/Ha/d) based on industry type. Light: 10,000 L/Ha/d. Heavy: 40,000+ L/Ha/d. AWWA M1
Institutional Demand Hospitals (per bed), Schools (per student). Hospitals: 400-600 L/bed/d. Schools: 45-100 L/student/d. Twort's Water Supply
Common Mistake: Sizing distribution mains strictly for Peak Hour Demand (PHD) in small residential cul-de-sacs. In loops serving less than 500 people, the Fire Flow + MDD scenario will almost always dictate the pipe size (typically driving it up to a minimum of 150mm / 6-inch pipe regardless of actual domestic demand).

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: An architect provides you with a landscape irrigation demand of 500 m³/day for a new district. Do you add this to the Peak Hour Demand to size your network?
Answer: No. Irrigation is a managed demand. I would specify in the design report and operational manual that irrigation systems must operate on automated timers during night-time off-peak hours (e.g., 11:00 PM to 4:00 AM). Adding irrigation to the domestic Peak Hour Demand (which occurs around 7:00 AM or 7:00 PM) would unnecessarily oversize the entire trunk network. It is added to the Average Day Demand for water source sizing, but not to the Peak Hour for pipe sizing.

4. Pipe Material Selection

Selecting the right pipe requires balancing hydraulic capacity, structural strength, corrosion resistance, and total lifecycle cost. No single material is perfect for all applications.

4.1 Ductile Iron (DI)
StandardsAWWA C150/151 BS EN 545
Pressure RatingPN16, PN25, PN40 (Very High)
CorrosionSusceptible to acidic soils. Requires internal cement-mortar lining and external zinc/bitumen or polyethylene encasement.
Surge ResistanceExcellent. Inherently handles high water hammer pressures.
Typical UseTrunk mains, pumped rising mains, high-traffic loads.
Failure ModeGraphitization (corrosion), localized pitting if coating fails.
4.2 High-Density Polyethylene (HDPE)
StandardsAWWA C906 ISO 4427 EN 12201
Pressure RatingPE100 typically rated up to PN16 or PN25 based on SDR (Standard Dimension Ratio).
CorrosionImmune to galvanic corrosion. Chemically inert.
Surge ResistanceVery good due to material flexibility (lower wave celerity reduces water hammer spikes).
Typical UseTrenchless installation (HDD), areas with soil settlement, aggressive soils.
Failure ModeSlow crack growth, failure at butt-fusion joints if welded improperly, permeation of hydrocarbons (do not use in contaminated soils).
4.3 Unplasticized Polyvinyl Chloride (uPVC / PVC-U)
StandardsAWWA C900 BS EN 1452
Pressure RatingPN10 to PN16. (Lower than DI).
CostHighly economical for diameters up to 300mm.
InstallationPush-fit joints. Requires careful bedding to prevent point loading.
LimitationsBecomes brittle with UV exposure; fatigue failure under cyclic pumping loads.
4.4 Glass Reinforced Plastic (GRP)
StandardsAWWA C950 ISO 10639
Pressure RatingUp to PN32, extremely customizable.
Typical UseVery large transmission mains (>1000mm) where steel is too expensive and DI is too heavy.
LimitationsHighly dependent on installation. If side-fill compaction is poor, the pipe deflects and joints fail.
4.5 Steel (Carbon Steel / Stainless)
StandardsAWWA C200 AWWA M11
AdvantagesUltimate strength. Welded joints prevent thrust block requirements (fully restrained).
LimitationsRequires strict Cathodic Protection (CP) and high-quality coatings (e.g., 3LPE). Expensive. Stainless is used primarily inside pump stations or WTPs.

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: You have a 400mm transmission main passing through an old industrial landfill site contaminated with petrol/hydrocarbons. What pipe material do you choose?
Answer: I absolutely cannot use HDPE or PVC. Hydrocarbons will permeate through polyethylene and contaminate the potable water supply. I must use Ductile Iron equipped with a specialized protective gasket (like Nitrile/NBR), or fully welded Steel. (Reference: AWWA M23 & M11 limitations).

5. Hydraulic Design

The core mathematics of water engineering. Gravity brings it down, pumps push it up, and friction takes a toll.

Hazen-Williams Equation (Most Common for Water)
$$V = 0.849 \cdot C \cdot R^{0.63} \cdot S^{0.54}$$
Headloss formulation: $$h_f = \frac{10.67 \cdot L \cdot Q^{1.852}}{C^{1.852} \cdot D^{4.87}}$$
Where: C = Roughness Coeff (140 for new HDPE/PVC, 130 for DI). Q in m³/s, D & L in meters.
Darcy-Weisbach Equation (More accurate, accounts for viscosity/temperature)
$$h_f = f \frac{L}{D} \frac{V^2}{2g}$$
Where: f = friction factor (calculated via Colebrook-White equation), V = velocity.

System Configurations & Nodes

Branched Systems: Dead-ends. High risk of stagnation. Cheaper. Only used where loops are geographically impossible.
Looped Systems: Two-way flow. Ensures redundancy, minimizes headloss, keeps water fresh. Required by Ten States Standards wherever feasible.

Common Mistake: Relying on the C-value of a new pipe. A Ductile Iron pipe might have a C=130 on day one, but after 30 years of tuberculation, C might drop to 100 or lower. All hydraulic models must be run using the "Design Life" C-value, not the factory C-value.

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: Why do we limit maximum velocity in water mains to 1.5 - 2.5 m/s?
Answer: Three reasons. First, Headloss is proportional to the square of the velocity (V²), meaning pumping costs increase exponentially at high velocities. Second, Water Hammer pressure surge magnitude is directly proportional to the change in velocity (Joukowsky equation): $$\Delta P = \rho a \Delta V$$ Third, extreme velocity causes scouring of internal cement linings in DI pipes and erosion of fittings.

6. Water Network Modeling

Translating equations into computational reality using EPANET or Bentley WaterGEMS.

Demand AllocationDistributing spatial demands to junctions. Methods include: Nearest Node, Thiessen Polygons, or Meter geocoding.
CalibrationAdjusting C-values, PRV settings, and baseline demands in the model until model pressures match real-world field hydrant tests.
EPS (Extended Period Simulation)Running the model over 24-48 hours. Required to verify that tanks fill during the night and empty during the day, and to calculate water age.
Validation ChecksEnsure all nodes > 2.5 bar. Ensure all pipes < 2.0 m/s. Check tank turnover (should exchange at least 20-30% of volume daily to maintain chlorine residuals).

💡 Interview Question Focus

Q: You run an EPS model. The elevated storage tank starts at 50% full at hour 0, but at hour 24 it is only 30% full. What does this mean, and how do you fix it?
Answer: This means the system is operating at a mass balance deficit; the supply pumps or source cannot keep up with the 24-hour Total Daily Demand. If left running, the tank will eventually run dry. To fix this, I must either 1) Increase the supply pump flow rate, 2) Decrease network demand/leakage, or 3) Check if the pump control rules (HGL on/off settings) are shutting the pump down prematurely before the tank can fill.

7. Final Deliverables (IFC Stage)

An engineer's job is not complete when the model runs; it is complete when the contractor can build it.

  • Design Report: Contains all criteria, population calcs, pump sizing, and hydraulic model outputs.
  • Plan & Profile Drawings: Show horizontal alignment, vertical clearance from sewers (Ten States mandates water must be 18 inches above sewers), and thrust block locations.
  • Standard Details: Trench bedding (Type 3, 4, 5 etc.), valve chambers, washout details, air valve installations.
  • Technical Specifications: Exact pipe material standards (e.g., "DI to conform to AWWA C151 class 53"), testing procedures (hydrostatic test at 1.5x working pressure).
  • BOQ (Bill of Quantities): Accurate take-offs for pipes, fittings (bends, tees), valves, and excavation volumes.
